# Founder Dilution and Control

Founder dilution and control is the principle that what actually decides a founder's economic outcome and decision power is *dilution and deal terms*, not the headline valuation founders tend to fixate on. Each funding round creates *new* shares for investors, so a founder's percentage ownership shrinks even though they never sell their own shares — owning 50 of 100 shares (50%) becomes 50 of 120 shares (~41%) once 20 new shares are issued, and this repeats every round until median founding-team ownership is around 23% by Series B. Layered on top of dilution are term-sheet clauses that govern *who gets paid first* and *who can veto what*, which means a high valuation can coexist with a founder who has lost both the majority of the economics and effective control of the company.

## Key Mechanics

- **Dilution is new shares, not sold shares**: investors get freshly issued shares, so the founder's slice of a larger pie shrinks without any sale on their part — and because it compounds across every round, ownership erodes steadily (median founding-team ownership ~23% by Series B), with the board increasingly able to override founder decisions.
- **Valuation is negotiated, not calculated**: early-stage valuations reflect perceived risk and investor interest rather than a formula, so fixating on the headline number misreads where the real leverage in a deal sits.
- **Liquidation preference — who gets paid first**: determines the payout order and amount on a sale. A *1x non-participating* preference returns the investor's money before common shareholders; a *participating* preference returns the money *and* a share of the remainder, materially changing what the founder nets in a modest exit.
- **Protective provisions — investor veto power**: give investors veto over major decisions (selling the company, raising new rounds, budget changes), so control over consequential moves can pass to investors regardless of the founder's nominal ownership.
- **Pro-rata rights**: let early investors maintain their ownership percentage by investing in future rounds, concentrating ongoing influence with the earliest backers.
- **Drag-along rights**: force minority shareholders to sell if the majority agrees to an acquisition, removing a dissenting founder's ability to block a sale.
- **Terms bite hardest in a crisis**: these clauses are most consequential when things go wrong — the source ties the forced resignation of a high-profile founder (Travis Kalanick) to exactly this shift of control from founder to investors when a company hits trouble.

## Tensions & Tradeoffs

- **It is the mechanical cost of the venture path**: dilution and control loss are precisely what `[[venture-scale-vs-durable-profitability]]` trades away in exchange for scale — so understanding these terms is how a founder knows *what they are actually giving up* when they choose to raise, and why the bridge-round trap (raising on worse terms under runway pressure) is so damaging.
- **Valuation is a distraction from control**: optimizing for a high headline valuation while ignoring liquidation preferences and protective provisions can leave a founder nominally "successful" yet economically thin and operationally powerless — the term sheet, not the valuation, is where the negotiation that matters happens.
- **Avoiding dilution entirely is now an option**: because dilution only accrues when one raises, the AI-enabled bootstrapping path (`[[bootstrapping-as-ai-enabled-alternative]]`) is the way to keep 100% ownership and full control — making these terms a cost a founder can now more often decline to pay rather than one inherent to building a company.
- **Vantage caveat**: the ownership percentages and term descriptions come from a founder-education video, so they are illustrative of the *structure* (dilution compounds, terms govern control) rather than universal figures — actual terms are negotiated per deal and vary widely.
