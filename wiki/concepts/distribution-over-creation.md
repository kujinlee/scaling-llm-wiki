# Distribution Over Creation

Distribution over creation is the strategic claim that once AI drives the cost of *building* — software, content, services — toward zero, the scarce resource is no longer the artifact but the *attention* directed at it. When anyone can vibe-code a working app or generate publishable content on demand, building stops being the differentiator and distribution becomes the moat. The practical inversion that follows is to build the minimum viable thing — a single sharp feature — and pour the overwhelming majority of effort into marketing, specifically into discovering a *repeatable viral format* that reliably converts attention into reach.

## Key Mechanics

- **One core feature, not a platform**: the first version should do exactly one valuable thing well (the corpus's example is a calorie tracker that reads a photo). Simplicity is not a compromise — it is what makes the product describable in a sentence, shareable by word of mouth, and easy for influencers to feature.
- **The 90% marketing rule**: after launch, distribution should consume the bulk of effort (the source's figure is ~90%). The default builder error is to over-invest in features and under-invest in getting seen — exactly backwards once building is cheap.
- **Find a repeatable viral format**: the goal of marketing is not one lucky hit but a *reusable template* (a content shape, hook, or format) that can be run again and again to produce reach predictably — a distribution analogue of turning a one-off success into a standing process.
- **Distribution channels are the new build surface**: short-form video, micro-influencer partnerships, and copied-then-personalized viral content are treated as the high-leverage work — the place skill and iteration are spent, the way code used to be.
- **Validate manually before automating distribution**: the source insists on manually validating content/output before automating its production — a graduated-trust discipline (`[[graduated-autonomy]]`) applied to the content pipeline, so the repeatable format is proven by hand before it is scaled.

## How It Appears in the Corpus

The Sabrina Ramonov "5 Ways to Make Money in 2026 (with AI)" tutorial frames nearly every monetization path around distribution rather than creation: building a personal AI brand by copying then personalizing viral short-form content; making faceless AI-avatar videos whose value is reach, not production difficulty; and — most explicitly — "vibe coding" a simple one-feature app and then dedicating 90% of effort to marketing, with the stated ultimate goal of finding a "repeatable viral format." Across the five methods the durable through-line is that AI makes the *making* easy, so the money is in the *getting seen*.

## Tensions & Tradeoffs

- **The market-side companion to `[[shifting-bottlenecks]]`**: where that concept tracks the engineering constraint migrating to verification and merge-trust, this tracks the *commercial* constraint migrating from building to distribution — same logic (automate the old hard part, the bottleneck moves), different axis.
- **It presupposes the floor that `[[vibe-coding-vs-agentic-engineering]]` and `[[software-3-0]]` describe**: cheap building is exactly the floor-raising effect that makes creation a commodity; this concept is the answer to "if everyone can build, what is now scarce?" — but it inherits the same hazard, since a fast-built, marketing-first product can ship functional-but-unsafe software whose distribution outpaces its quality.
- **Distinct from `[[founder-wedge-strategy]]`**: the wedge strategy wins by *deep domain embedding* and replacing a painful workflow end to end; distribution-over-creation wins by *attention capture* on a light, broadly-shareable artifact. They can conflict — depth-of-embedding versus breadth-of-reach are different routes to revenue, and a viral-format business has little of the defensibility a forward-deployed wedge builds.
- **A repeatable viral format is a fragile moat**: distribution advantages decay as formats saturate and platforms shift, so "build light, market hard" trades durable product depth for speed of reach — strong for fast monetization, weak as a long-run defensibility story.
- **Vantage caveat**: the claims come from a beginner-oriented monetization tutorial, so the specific tactics and figures (90% marketing, named tools) are illustrative rules of thumb, not measured results; the durable part is the creation-to-distribution constraint shift, not any single channel.
