# Founder Wedge Strategy

The founder wedge strategy is a go-to-market discipline for the AI era: rather than launching a broad platform, a founder *wedges* into a single painful, messy, repetitive workflow, embeds deeply with customers as a "forward-deployed engineer," automates that workflow end-to-end with AI, and "goes undercover" to become a genuine domain expert. The bet is that vast "white space" exists outside core computer science — back office, finance, data, academics, cybersecurity, customer service — where full AI solutions (not mere copilots) can capture an industry, leaving room for hundreds of new AI unicorns.

## Key Mechanics

- **Wedge, don't sprawl**: pick one acutely painful workflow and win it completely; depth in a narrow seam beats breadth across a platform.
- **Forward-deploy with customers**: embed on-site to learn the messy operational reality and automate the repetitive, tedious tasks — the same forward-deployed-engineer posture that drives `[[ontology-operating-system]]` adoption at Palantir.
- **Go undercover to gain domain expertise**: the founder becomes the expert whose tacit knowledge is then distilled into the skills/runbooks an `[[ai-native-company]]` runs on.
- **Full AI solution, not a copilot**: the winning product replaces the workflow rather than assisting it — driving real systems end to end (overlapping `[[computer-use-automation]]` and `[[agentic-issue-resolution]]`).
- **White space is the opportunity**: non-CS industries are underserved; the moment is framed as the best in history to start a company, with cited YC batches averaging 3x growth in 3 months.

## How It Appears in the Corpus

The Garry Tan / Diana Hu Stanford CS153 lecture's founder section presents the wedge approach directly: identify a painful workflow, embed deeply with customers as a forward-deploy engineer, and automate the repetitive and messy parts. It cites Salient (loan-servicing agents) and Happy Robot (freight forwarding) reaching eight-figure revenue within a year, often by founders who "go undercover" to become domain experts, and names the white-space industries beyond CS where hundreds of AI unicorns could emerge.

## Tensions & Tradeoffs

- **Requires real understanding, not just automation**: going undercover only pays off if the founder genuinely internalizes the domain — the `[[thinking-vs-understanding]]` caveat, since automating a workflow you do not understand misfires confidently.
- **The FDE bargain cuts both ways**: embedding to capture a customer's know-how is the same dynamic that makes `[[ontology-operating-system]]` adoption powerful and raises the same vendor-dependency / know-how-exposure question for the customer being automated.
- **Vantage-dependent claims**: the growth figures and "best time in history" framing come from a YC investor's seat — a directional thesis about opportunity, not a guarantee for any given founder.
