# AI-Native vs. AI-Assisted

AI-native vs. AI-assisted is the diagnostic distinction between two fundamentally different ways an organization can adopt AI. An **AI-native** organization runs its workflows *entirely* through AI with minimal human intervention — agents are the default doers, humans the exception. An **AI-assisted** organization keeps human-run workflows and uses AI to *enhance* them — AI is a faster tool inside a process people still own. The pointed claim is that the gap between the two is large and widely misrepresented: many companies *claim* to be AI-native while being merely AI-assisted, because true native operation is gated less by ambition than by readiness — clean data infrastructure and leadership that actually understands the shift.

## Key Mechanics

- **A spectrum, not a badge**: native and assisted are the two poles of how deeply AI is wired into operations — native = workflows are AI-driven end to end; assisted = AI accelerates tasks humans still perform — and most organizations sit closer to the assisted pole than their self-description admits.
- **The native bar is minimal human intervention**: the defining test is whether work *completes* through agents without a human in each loop, the closed-loop operation that an `[[ai-native-company]]` and a `[[dark-factory]]` embody — not merely whether AI tools are present.
- **Readiness gates the transition**: the binding constraints on becoming genuinely native are *clean data infrastructure* (without structured, trustworthy data the agents have nothing reliable to act on) and *leadership understanding* (without it the org adopts tools without rewiring the workflow) — so "AI-native" is an organizational-change problem, not a procurement one.
- **"AI-washing" as the common failure**: claiming native status while operating assisted is the prevalent pattern, a credibility gap that the distinction exists to expose.

## Tensions & Tradeoffs

- **It sharpens `[[ai-native-company]]`'s closed-loop vs. open-loop framing into a readiness test**: where that concept describes the closed-loop org and its payoff, this distinction supplies the diagnostic — *are your workflows actually AI-driven end to end?* — and names data infrastructure and leadership as the gates, the same `[[ontology-operating-system]]` precondition that structured, trustworthy data must exist before agents can run on it.
- **Native operation removes the friction that carried judgment**: minimal-human-intervention workflows are exactly where `[[friction-as-judgment]]` warns the human review checkpoint disappears, so a genuinely native org must rebuild quality signal mechanically (`[[custom-eval-systems]]`) rather than assume it — native is not automatically better, only more automated.
- **Most should be assisted on purpose**: the distinction is descriptive, not an exhortation to rush to native — `[[graduated-autonomy]]` argues for widening AI authority only as trust is earned, so being honestly AI-assisted can be the correct, safer state rather than a failure to be ashamed of.
- **Vantage caveat**: the "most claimed-native are actually assisted" judgment is an asserted observation from a discussion podcast, not a survey — the durable idea is the native/assisted readiness distinction, not a measured prevalence.
