# Taiwan's DRAM Failure

**Channel:** Asianometry | **Duration:** 37:55 | **URL:** https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=ehT3U935Pww

> [!summary] Quick Reference
> **TL;DR:** This video chronicles Taiwan's two-decade-long, multi-billion dollar struggle and ultimate failure to establish a globally competitive DRAM memory industry.
>
> **Key Takeaways:**
> - Recognize DRAM as a highly volatile, capital-intensive commodity market, distinct from logic chips.
> - Understand that licensing core technology without owning IP limits margins and strategic control.
> - Be aware that government guarantees can create moral hazard, encouraging excessively risky ventures.
> - Learn how market oversupply driven by past shortages (bullwhip effect) can cause catastrophic price crashes.
> - Expect consolidation as an inevitable outcome in highly cyclical and competitive industries like DRAM.
>

## 1. Early Semiconductor Successes and the Lure of DRAM
Taiwan established its semiconductor prowess in the 1970s through ITRI, licensing CMOS process from RCA. This led to the founding of successful logic chip foundries like UMC and TSMC, which provided custom ASICs for Taiwan's electronics industry. However, Taiwan's strength was primarily in logic chips, with little presence in the notoriously difficult DRAM memory sector despite early, unsuccessful attempts like Quasel in the 1980s.

---

## 2. The 1988 DRAM Shortage and Taiwan's Renewed Efforts
A severe global DRAM shortage in 1988, driven by increased PC demand and supply-side issues, highlighted memory's critical importance to Taiwan's electronics ecosystem. This scarcity pressured Taiwanese companies and the government to re-enter the DRAM market. Acer Group launched TI-Acer in partnership with Texas Instruments, while ITRI initiated the ambitious Submicron Project, leading to the spinoff of Vanguard International Semiconductor (Vanguard) in 1994.

---

## 3. The Mid-1990s Gold Rush and Devastating Crash
Following TI-Acer and Vanguard, a "DRAM gold rush" ensued, with private startups like Nanya, Powerchip, and ProMOS, and established players like Winbond and Macronix, entering the market. Many licensed IP from fading Japanese memory makers, often taking on significant debt. Initial optimism driven by the Windows 95 supercycle saw high profits in 1994-1995. However, this led to massive overcapacity, triggering a brutal price crash from late 1995 through 1998, a classic "bullwhip effect" that caused immense financial losses and forced exits or pivots for many firms, including TI-Acer (acquired by TSMC) and Vanguard (pivoting to logic foundry).

---

## 4. The "Two Trillion" Policy and the 2008 Financial Crisis
In the early 2000s, to counter economic sluggishness and capital flight, the Taiwanese government launched the "Two Trillion and Twin Stars" policy, explicitly designating DRAM as a key sector for growth with significant government support. This spurred a new wave of fab construction, with companies like Nanya (forming Inotera with Infineon/Qimonda), ProMOS, Powerchip, and Winbond taking on more debt. Another boom in 2006-2007, fueled by expected Windows Vista demand and Chinese PC market growth, further encouraged investment. However, this was short-lived, with prices crashing again in 2007, exacerbated by the 2008 Global Financial Crisis. Taiwanese firms absorbed 42% of the industry's losses despite holding only 13-15% market share, pushed to the brink of collapse.

---

## 5. The Failed Taiwan Memory Company and Industry Consolidation
Facing a potential systemic crisis, the Taiwanese government proposed the Taiwan Memory Company (TMC) in 2009, aiming to merge major domestic DRAM makers, secure Elpida's technology, and build a globally competitive entity. This plan met public criticism over taxpayer money and, crucially, resistance from the very companies it sought to consolidate due to differing technologies and a preference for direct bailouts. As a precarious market recovery emerged, the urgency waned, and the Legislative Yuan eventually rejected the project. In the aftermath, ProMOS and Powerchip pivoted to logic foundries, while Micron consolidated much of Taiwan's remaining DRAM capacity by acquiring Elpida (and thus Rexchip) and Inotera, making Taiwan a major hub for Micron's global DRAM production.

---

## Conclusion
Taiwan's two-decade pursuit of a leading position in the DRAM industry ended largely in failure, despite billions spent and numerous government initiatives. The core reasons included the extreme volatility and capital intensity of the DRAM commodity market, a reliance on licensed foreign technology that limited IP ownership and margins, the "bullwhip effect" of demand-supply cycles, and a moral hazard created by implicit government guarantees. While TSMC and UMC thrived in logic, the DRAM market ultimately consolidated into a few global giants, with Taiwan's indigenous efforts largely absorbed or redirected, leaving Nanya Technology as the sole remaining major domestic DRAM player.
