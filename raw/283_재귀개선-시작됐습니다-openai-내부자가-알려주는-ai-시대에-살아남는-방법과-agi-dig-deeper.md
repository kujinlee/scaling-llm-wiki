## Defining AI-Native Startups and OpenAI's Role

When asked how a startup should think about building a competitive "moat," Wolfie Bain suggests founders should focus on one of two things: either solving a real, specific problem they deeply understand, or building something very general about intelligence. The clear implication is that startups should focus on the former, as OpenAI's core mission is to tackle the latter.

Bain reveals that before joining the company, he was skeptical and thought the AGI (Artificial General Intelligence) mission was "all marketing." Now, he insists he "really, really believe[s]" that the entire company is singularly focused on achieving AGI. This mission informs their strategy of acting as a foundational intelligence utility for others to build upon.

The conversation then turns to what makes a company truly "AI-native." Bain argues it's not about a particular technology but is "almost an attitude thing." The word he feels best captures this is "tinkerer"—a culture of constant, hands-on experimentation with the technology.

He points to OpenAI's own accelerating development cycle as an example of this in action. Where a new model might have been released every 15 months two years ago, the pace has quickened to a new release approximately every six weeks. The reason for this speed, he explains, is a powerful feedback loop: "we're using our own tools to build our own tools." He gets visibly excited describing this as "using GPT to create GPT," noting that they are now seeing "small sparks of recursive self-improvement."

The speakers then formally introduce themselves. Marc Manera leads the startups team at OpenAI. A former founder who also worked at Amazon Web Services, his team's focus is to support startups that are using OpenAI's technology to build their companies and products. Wolfie Bain leads the *applied AI* side of that same team. His group gets very "hands-on technically," helping startups with specific challenges like building agents, creating evaluation systems, and fine-tuning models. Bain, also a former founder, notes that the team has many ex-founders because they "really, really love startups; it's in our core."

## Real-World Applications and Advanced Codex Usage

OpenAI employees Marc and Linus describe how Codex has become an indispensable tool in their daily work, extending far beyond its initial purpose as a coding assistant.

Marc uses Codex for a variety of tasks, particularly data analysis. For example, he can ask it in natural language to analyze product usage trends, such as identifying which startups are scaling their use of OpenAI's models or getting a breakdown of how many startups are using the models in each country. He also relies on it to manage and synthesize feedback during a new model's alpha testing phase. Instead of manually sifting through numerous Slack channels and documents, he has Codex summarize the detailed feedback from all the testing partners. He might ask, "Every day I want an output of every startup that has tested the model, what their specific feedback has been," which allows him to stay current on progress and identify areas for improvement. This data also feeds back into development, where Codex assists in coding and building applications on top of these insights.

Linus notes that his own use of Codex has evolved significantly. He initially used it just for coding tasks like building prototypes and websites. It then became a powerful tool for production engineering and software development. Now, he uses it for "everything." For complex research, he can instruct Codex to "spin up many, many sub-agents, do that all in parallel, and then bring me back the findings." Most strikingly, his interaction method has changed. Instead of typing, he often speaks to Codex, a habit his flatmate finds a bit strange. While cooking or doing other things, he can simply say, "Codex, can you program this?" or "Can you do some research?" and let it work in the background while he continues his task. This represents a major shift from a traditional, synchronous workflow to an asynchronous, conversational one.

They offer several tips for power users.
*   **Automations:** Marc frequently uses automations for recurring tasks. The daily feedback summary is a perfect example; he can set it to run at 7:00 AM every day to get a fresh report without any manual intervention.
*   **Computer Use:** A recently added feature allows Codex to operate programs on your computer and the web. It can test an application you just built or create a note in a note-taking app. This happens in the background, so the user retains full control of their mouse and keyboard to continue working on other things.
*   **Automated Refactoring:** Linus suggests a valuable workflow for developers: running a periodic automation to refactor their codebase. The automation can analyze recent pull requests, identify common patterns, and suggest what should be extracted into reusable functions or components. This turns the often-painful task of maintenance into a continuous, manageable process.
*   **Codex Mobile:** The mobile app is especially powerful when combined with the "computer use" feature. It allows Linus to access and control his laptop from his phone when he's away from his desk. He can set up automations, write scripts, or find files on his computer, saving him significant time and hassle.

Finally, they emphasize the importance of integration. A great starting point for any new user is to connect Codex to essential tools like Slack, Gmail, and Google Calendar. Once connected, it can perform tasks like finding a specific Slack message from memory ("I know this person said something about this topic... find that thread") or refactoring a busy schedule ("Can you refactor my calendar for next week? I've got too many meetings stacked up").

## The Impact of AI on Startup Structure and Human Capital

AI is enabling a new paradigm where startups can achieve significant scale while remaining intentionally small. One speaker notes a trend of companies staying smaller by headcount, not by revenue or usage. He provides an extreme example of a stealth consumer company with two founders who have raised funding but are actively trying *not* to hire anyone. Their goal is to build the entire business using AI agents powered by models like Codex for all foundational parts of the company. They are testing how long they can operate this way—through building the first version of the product, launching it, acquiring users, and even automating customer support.

While they will likely hire eventually, the ability to stay small for longer is seen as a strategic advantage. It eliminates the internal administrative overhead and organizational layers that come with a larger staff, which can be counterproductive to the speed required in a startup's early days. While companies selling into the enterprise still need a human component for sales and customer success, this lean model is becoming increasingly viable for others.

This shift is amplified by AI's ability to multiply an individual's productivity. A speaker suggests that an engineer who uses AI well is no longer just a "10x engineer" but can become a "100x engineer." This has a dual effect: while AI can perform many jobs, potentially reducing the need for staff, it also means that each talented person a company *does* hire can produce vastly more output. The dynamic balances out, as a small, highly-leveraged team can accomplish what once required a much larger organization.

This trend is particularly exciting for solopreneurs and smaller businesses. Previously, building a passion project or side hustle was often impossible without hiring a developer and securing external funding. Now, with AI handling tasks like coding via Codex, marketing automation, and sales, individuals can build and run a "really cool business" on their own. The business may not be on a hypergrowth trajectory, but it can be viable and sustainable.

An example given is of founders in the London startup scene building outbound sales tools. Creating such a product requires a deep understanding of customer communication and tone of voice, as well as the technical ability to build the tool. In the past, this meant hiring a team to talk to customers and a separate team of engineers to build. Now, a single person, even one who would have previously been considered "non-technical," can handle the entire process. They can speak directly with customers to understand their needs and then use AI to turn that insight into a functional AI agent that performs outbound sales, which can then be sold as a SaaS product. This eliminates the need to choose between building the product or talking to customers; founders can now do both, enabling a new wave of small-scale, highly effective startups.

## Staying Ahead in a Rapidly Evolving AI Landscape

Within the last year, a fundamental step change has occurred in AI, shifting the paradigm from "reasoning" to "agents." Previously, while models were getting smarter at reasoning, building them into autonomous agents was difficult; founders had to construct careful, brittle workflows to guide the product, and it was hard to trust the models to operate successfully for long durations.

In the last nine months, however, model capabilities have advanced to the point where they can run for much longer, use tools reliably, and self-correct when they go off track. This allows startups to build "real agents" that can handle complicated, long-running tasks. Instead of micromanaging the model with rigid workflows, founders can now build a "harness" and let the model itself act as the intelligence engine. This shift is enabling agentic applications across numerous domains, including law, finance, and enterprise, with coding (via products like Codex) being the first area where this became a reality.

Despite this rapid technological evolution, the core principles of building a successful startup remain unchanged. It's still fundamentally about obsessing over a customer and solving their problem. AI is an incredible new tool that changes *how* a problem is solved, but it doesn't change the foundational need to solve a real problem for a real customer.

This dynamic addresses a common fear among founders, often memed as "OpenAI is killing my startup." When a startup's moat is its deep, specialized understanding of a specific customer workflow—be it in manufacturing, law, or finance—it isn't competing with a general-purpose platform like OpenAI. OpenAI's goal is to be an "intelligence utility," providing the foundational tools for specialists to build upon.

To thrive in this environment, founders should adopt two key strategies:
1.  **Focus on vertical problems.** Don't try to build general intelligence, as that is OpenAI's "north star." Instead, build a product for a specific niche you understand deeply.
2.  **Build with the future in mind.** The best startups operate with their "head six months in the future," betting that the underlying models will continue to improve. They architect their products so that when a new, more powerful model is released, their product instantly gets better. This allows them to catalyze on platform improvements, and their growth goes "through the roof." This requires a willingness to discard previous approaches as new capabilities make them obsolete.

Being "AI native" is less about a specific technology and more about an attitude of being a "tinkerer." AI-native companies encourage their teams to constantly experiment, integrating AI into "no-regrets" processes where the upside is high and the downside is low. For example, using AI to write code is a good trade-off; it saves time, and if the output isn't good, it can be discarded. The same applies to triaging bug reports or customer support issues. This methodical approach of examining every part of the business and asking "Where can we start with AI here?" is key.

This new way of working is also blurring the lines between traditional roles. The most effective teams are seeing the emergence of a "hybrid profile": a single, product-oriented, customer-facing, and highly technical individual who can own a process end-to-end—from idea and design to prototyping, marketing copy, and deployment.

## Cultivating an AI-Native Culture and Organizational Structure

One of the most interesting parts of joining OpenAI was the onboarding process, which sets the company's AI-native tone from the start. Traditionally, getting up to speed at a new company is a long process involving reading documents, trying to find information in different silos, and feeling nervous about bothering colleagues with questions. At OpenAI, however, new hires are immediately immersed in an environment where AI is the primary tool for finding information. By connecting tools like Codex and ChatGPT to all of the company's knowledge stores—including Notion, Slack, and Google Drive—employees can find answers quickly and efficiently. This fundamentally changes their workflow habits. When a question arises, their first instinct is to turn to AI, which immediately demonstrates its value and boosts their productivity, creating a powerful feedback loop. The speaker notes that before joining, he thought ChatGPT was "really cool," but upon seeing it fully integrated into the company's operations, he realized, "Wow, I've not been using it anywhere near to its potential." The goal is to make AI the "first choice" for every employee from their first day, ensuring they become AI-native for the rest of their time at the company.

This approach fosters a culture of experimentation, or as one speaker puts it, it allows employees to be "tinkerers." To accelerate this shift, organizations should identify and empower the small percentage of employees who are natural early adopters. These are the "builders" and "champions" who have been using tools like Codex and ChatGPT from day one, pressure-testing new features, and finding novel ways to change their own work habits. By amplifying these individuals, they can become internal leaders who help their entire departments get "halfway up the curve" of AI adoption. For example, OpenAI ran an internal Codex hackathon led by one passionate team member, and everyone on the team learned from them. This grassroots approach helps spread expertise and enthusiasm throughout the organization.

The ultimate goal is to integrate AI into every facet of the business. At OpenAI, AI is used in "pretty much everything." The company's rapid development pace—releasing new models every six weeks, compared to every 15 months just two years ago—is a direct result of using their own tools to build new ones, a process described as "recursive self-improvement." They use Codex to write code for Codex and use GPT models to conduct research for the next generation of GPT. This compounding effect of "many small uses" is seen across the company. The strategic finance team uses AI to help raise and deploy capital, and non-engineering teams build their own custom internal applications to support go-to-market operations, creating purpose-built tools that are more effective than off-the-shelf software.

This deep integration of AI is also changing the structure of companies. Organizations are becoming flatter and more agile. AI can automate many of the reporting and information-consolidation tasks previously done by middle managers, allowing for flatter hierarchies and wider spans of control, with some managers overseeing 30 or 40 direct reports. Roles are also beginning to blur. The traditional tension between product and engineering teams diminishes as product managers can start to build prototypes themselves using tools like Codex, and engineers are empowered to make more product and design decisions. This fosters more coherent and collaborative teams. A key structural trend is the rise of "forward-deployed engineering teams"—technical teams that work directly with customers. By being close to the customer on a technical level, they can understand problems, gather feedback, and translate that directly into real product improvements, creating a very tight and efficient iteration loop.
