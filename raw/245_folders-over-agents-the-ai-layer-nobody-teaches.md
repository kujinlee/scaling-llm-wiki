## 1. The Peril of Outdated AI Learning
▶ [0:08–1:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uYRXYsPS66g&t=8s)
Many AI courses, prompt trainings, and frameworks teach at the wrong abstraction layer, leading to constant relearning and outdated knowledge within months. This creates a feeling of falling behind, as new tools and workflows constantly emerge. The focus on transient features and superficial methods means that learners are often chasing fleeting trends rather than building durable skills.

---

## 2. Challenging the Myth of Rapid AI Advancement
▶ [1:36–9:37](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uYRXYsPS66g&t=96s)
The perception that AI is moving too fast is a misconception. Core AI concepts, like transformer models, have existed for nearly a decade, and the underlying software fundamentals have been around since the 1950s. The real reason people feel behind isn't the speed of AI, but rather learning at the wrong, non-foundational layer that doesn't survive model or framework updates.

---

## 3. Introducing the Interpretable Context Methodology (ICM)
▶ [9:37–10:26](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uYRXYsPS66g&t=577s)
The speaker introduces his "Interpretable Context Methodology" (ICM), encapsulated by the tagline "Folders over Agents." This approach emphasizes that the way information is structured and context is organized within file systems and databases is more critical than the specific AI models or agentic frameworks used. ICM provides a stable architectural foundation for AI systems.

---

## 4. The Enduring Power of Contextual Architecture
▶ [10:26–13:10](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=uYRXYsPS66g&t=626s)
Unlike agentic frameworks or specific tools that can become obsolete in 90 days, a robust architecture and context system survives and even improves with new AI model updates. By organizing information across five layers (Identity, Routing, Stage Contract, Reference Material, Working Artifacts), systems become model-agnostic and scalable, ensuring long-term utility and control over outcomes.
